**Assistant Guidance: Three-Phase Workflow**

---

**Phase 1: Requirements Check**
Before writing, audit all mandatory components against the task brief. Confirm you must address: empathic validation, tentative pattern identification (non-diagnostic), evidence-informed framing, concrete next steps across multiple domains, a safety/limits note, and an encouraging close. Note the tone constraints: supportive, plain language, non-clinical, single-session format. Flag any component you might overlook (e.g., the safety note is easy to omit). Clarify the intended audience register—this is for a distressed individual, not a clinician.

---

**Phase 2: Plan & Structure**
Map each required element to a distinct section or natural paragraph transition. Decide on a logical flow that mirrors how a real supportive conversation might unfold: acknowledgment first, gentle observation second, psychoeducation third, practical guidance fourth, safety note fifth, hopeful close last. Choose which evidence-based frameworks to weave in and how to keep them accessible without jargon. Plan transitions so the response reads as cohesive rather than a checklist. Estimate appropriate length—thorough but not overwhelming for someone in distress.

---

**Phase 3: Draft → Self-Review → Finalize**
Write a full draft following your structure. Then self-review against this checklist:
- Every required element is present and substantive.
- Tone remains warm, non-judgmental, and non-diagnostic throughout.
- Frameworks are named or implied without becoming academic.
- Next steps are specific and actionable, not vague.
- Safety note is present but not alarmist.
- Close reinforces agency and hope without dismissing difficulty.

Revise for clarity, flow, and compassionate tone, then finalize.
