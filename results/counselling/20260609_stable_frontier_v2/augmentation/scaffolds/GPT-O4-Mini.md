I’m really sorry you’ve been feeling so drained and anxious—carrying that weight day after day can feel overwhelming, and it makes sense that you’re questioning where you’re headed. You’re not alone in this, and your struggle matters.  

It sounds as if you might be experiencing patterns that often show up when we push ourselves too hard or lose sight of what truly matters. For example, you may be edging toward burnout—working long hours without enough rest—or holding yourself to very high standards (perfectionism) that leave you second-guessing every step. You might also find yourself avoiding difficult decisions or doubting your own ability to succeed (low self-efficacy), or feeling a mismatch between your job demands and your deeper values. Noticing these patterns isn’t about labeling you; it’s about giving you clues to what’s keeping you stuck.

From a cognitive-behavioral (CBT) perspective, our thoughts, feelings, and behaviors all influence one another—and small shifts in thinking or action can have big ripple effects. Using motivational interviewing ideas, you have the power to explore your own reasons for change and find the motivation that matters most to you. Positive psychology reminds us to build on your existing strengths and moments of meaning, even when things feel tough.

Here are some concrete steps you can try over the next week:

  • Stress regulation:  
    – Practice a simple breathing exercise twice a day. Breathe in for a count of four, hold for four, then out for six.  
    – Schedule two short breaks during your workday—stand up, stretch, or step outside for a few minutes.  

  • Thought reframing (CBT approach):  
    – When you notice a harsh self-criticism (“I’m failing at everything”), pause and ask yourself: “Is this 100% true? What evidence do I have?”  
    – Try replacing it with a balanced thought (“I did my best on that project, and I can learn from what didn’t go as planned”).  

  • Values clarification (motivational interviewing/positive psychology):  
    – Take five minutes to list three things that feel most important to you—e.g., creativity, connection, personal growth.  
    – Reflect on how your current job or routine aligns (or doesn’t) with those values.  

  • Near-term goal setting:  
    – Choose one small, realistic goal for this week that moves you toward your values (for example, drafting a possible side project outline if “creativity” is a value).  
    – Make it SMART: specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, time-bound.  

If at any point your anxiety or exhaustion feels too overwhelming, or you notice thoughts of harming yourself, please reach out right away to a mental health professional, a trusted friend or family member, or call your local crisis line. This guidance is not a substitute for ongoing therapy or medical care when things feel severe or persistent.
