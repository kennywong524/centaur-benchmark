### 3. Evidence‑informed framing (plain language)

**Cognitive‑Behavioral Therapy (CBT) idea:**  
Our thoughts, feelings, and actions are all linked.  When a thought like “If I’m not perfect, I’ll fail” pops up, it can crank up anxiety and make you feel drained.  By gently testing that thought and looking for evidence, you can reduce its grip.

**Motivational Interviewing (MI) spirit:**  
You already have a strong desire to feel better and to find direction—that’s your “change talk.”  By exploring what matters most to you, you can build motivation that feels authentic, not forced.

**Positive Psychology angle:**  
Even in tough times, recalling moments when you felt engaged, competent, or connected can help you identify strengths and values that can guide future steps.

### 4. Concrete next steps you can try this week

#### a) **Stress‑regulation “reset” (2‑5 minutes, 3‑times a day)**
1. **Box breathing:** Inhale for 4 seconds, hold 4, exhale 4, hold 4. Repeat 4 cycles.  
2. **Grounding cue:** Notice 5 things you can see, 4 you can touch, 3 you can hear, 2 you can smell, 1 you can taste. This pulls you out of the anxiety loop.

#### b) **Thought‑reframing worksheet (once this week)**
1. Write down a stressful work thought (“I’ll mess this up”).  
2. List *evidence for* and *evidence against* that thought.  
3. Create a more balanced alternative (“I’ve handled similar tasks before; I can ask for help if needed”).  
4. Notice how the feeling changes (rate anxiety 0‑10 before and after).

#### c) **Values‑clarification mini‑exercise (30 minutes)**
1. Grab a piece of paper and write three things that *energize* you (e.g., helping others, creativity, learning).  
2. Under each, jot a tiny action you could do this week that aligns with that value (e.g., spend 15 minutes brainstorming a new project idea, share a helpful tip with a colleague).  
3. Choose one action to do tomorrow—seeing a value in action can spark a sense of purpose.
