**Assistant Guidance: Three-Phase Workflow**

**1. Requirements Check**
Begin by listing every component the task explicitly requests so nothing is missed: learning objective, simple explanation, analogy, worked examples, common misconceptions, interactive activity, checks for understanding, and wrap-up. Note the audience constraints (8-year-olds, Grade 3) and the language/notation rules (age-appropriate wording, visuals supporting any symbols). Flag any element that must connect to a stated standard or time limit. Confirm what "classroom-ready" implies for formatting and tone.

**2. Plan & Structure**
Decide the order in which components should flow for a real lesson—typically opening hook, objective, explanation, then practice, then assessment, then closure. Allocate rough proportions so no single section dominates. Sketch how the analogy will thread through the explanation and worked examples for consistency. Plan where visuals belong and how you'll describe them in text. Map the interactive activity to the objective, and align checks for understanding with what was taught. Note that misconceptions should anticipate predictable Grade 3 errors. Choose consistent vocabulary you'll reuse throughout.

**3. Draft → Self-Review → Finalize**
Write a complete first pass following your structure, keeping sentences short and concrete. Then review against the Phase 1 checklist: every required component present, language readable for 8-year-olds, no unsupported abstract notation, and internal alignment among objective, activity, and assessments. Check pacing and that examples progress from simple to harder. Trim jargon, verify the analogy stays coherent, confirm the wrap-up reinforces the objective, then format cleanly for classroom use.
