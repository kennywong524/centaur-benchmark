Lesson Segment: Improper Fractions and Mixed Numbers (Grade 3, 8-year-olds)

1. Learning Objective  
   By the end of this lesson, students will be able to  
   • recognize improper fractions (where the top number is bigger than the bottom)  
   • change an improper fraction into a mixed number using pictures  

2. Simple Explanation  
   • Fraction reminder: A fraction is a way to show parts of a whole. The bottom number (denominator) tells how many equal parts the whole is cut into. The top number (numerator) tells how many parts we have.  
   • Improper fraction: when the numerator is larger than the denominator, it means you have more parts than fit in just one whole.  
   • Mixed number: a number made of a whole number and a fraction.  

3. Analogy (Pizza Party!)  
   • Imagine you have pizzas cut into 4 equal slices each.  
   • If you have 1 whole pizza and then 1 extra slice, you have eaten 1 1/4 pizzas.  
   • If you eat 5 slices out of 4-slice pizzas, that is 5/4 slices eaten—this is an improper fraction because you ate more slices than one whole pizza has. You can also say 1 1/4 pizzas.  

4. Worked Examples (with Drawings)  
   Example A: 5/4  
     a. Draw two circles (pizzas), each divided into 4 slices.  
     b. Shade 5 slices in total.  
     c. Count shaded slices: you filled one whole pizza (4/4) and have 1 extra slice (1/4).  
     d. So 5/4 = 1 1/4.  

   Example B: 7/3 (using bars or cakes divided into 3 pieces)  
     a. Draw three rectangles, each cut into 3 equal parts.  
     b. Shade 7 parts.  
     c. You fill two whole bars (6/3 + 6/3) but you only had to shade 7 total, so that’s 2 whole bars (6 pieces) plus 1 extra piece.  
     d. 7/3 = 2 1/3.  

5. Common Misconceptions  
   • “Improper fraction means it’s wrong.” No—it just means “more than one whole.”  
   • Mixing up numerator and denominator: always check the bottom is size of each piece; the top is how many you have.  
   • Thinking you can only shade from one pizza—remember you can use as many wholes as you need!  

6. Interactive Activity (Pizza Strip Relay, 10 minutes)  
   Materials per pair: paper pizza strips (each strip is one pizza cut into 4 slices), scissors, glue sticks, blank paper.  
   Steps:  
     a. Each pair cuts out two pizza strips.  
     b. Teacher calls out an improper fraction (e.g., 6/4, 3/4, 9/4).  
     c. Partners shade the correct number of slices, then glue strips onto their paper to show whole pizzas and leftover slices.  
     d. Under the picture, they write the mixed number (e.g., 6/4 = 1 2/4).  

7. Checks for Understanding  
   • Thumbs-Up/Thumbs-Down: “5/4 is more than one. Thumbs up if you agree.”  
   • Turn-and-Talk: “Explain to your friend why 5/4 is 1 1/4.”  
   • Exit Ticket (3 problems):  
     1) Shade and convert 8/4  
     2) Shade and convert 10/4  
     3) Shade and convert 11/3  

8. Wrap-Up (2 minutes)  
   • Review: “An improper fraction has more pieces than one whole. We can draw it, count the wholes, then write the rest as a fraction to make a mixed number.”
