### **Grade 3 Lesson Segment: Pizza Party Fractions!**

**Learning Objective:** Students will be able to identify improper fractions and mixed numbers using pictures and will understand that they both represent amounts greater than one whole.

**Materials:**
*   Whiteboard or chart paper
*   Markers
*   Pre-drawn pizza circle templates (or other familiar circular fraction models) divided into halves, thirds, and fourths. Multiple copies per student/group.
*   Scissors (blunt-tip)
*   Glue sticks
*   Construction paper

---

#### **1. Simple Explanation & Analogy (5-7 mins)**

**(Teacher stands at the front with a whiteboard)**

"Good morning, mathematicians! We've been amazing at finding parts of a whole, like ½ a sandwich or ¾ of a pizza. But what happens at a pizza party when you're *really* hungry? You might eat more than just one slice from one pizza!

Today, we're learning about special kinds of fractions we use when we have more than one whole pizza! We call these **mixed numbers** and **improper fractions**.

**Analogy:** Think of your family's shoe rack.
*   A **mixed number** is like having some **whole pairs** of shoes and one **single shoe** from another pair. We might say, "We have **2 whole pairs** and **1 single shoe**." We write it as a whole number next to a fraction: **2 ½**.
*   An **improper fraction** is like counting *all the single shoes*. We don't care about pairs yet, we just count the total number of shoes. If we have 5 single shoes, we write it as **5/2** (5 shoes, and it takes 2 to make a pair).

They are just two different ways to talk about the same amount!"

---

#### **2. Worked Examples (10 mins)**

**(Teacher draws on the board, using circles for pizzas)**

"Let's see this with our pizza party!

**Example 1:**
> "I ate one whole pizza and another half of a pizza."
> *Draws 1 full circle and ½ of another circle.*
> "As a **mixed number**, we say **1 and ½**. We write it like this: **1 ½**."
> "Now, let's count all the *slices*. If each pizza was cut into 2 halves, how many halves do I have? One whole pizza is **2 halves**. Plus the other **1 half**. That's **3 halves** total! So, the **improper fraction** is **3/2**."
> "**1 ½** and **3/2** are the same amount of pizza!"

**Example 2:**
> "You ate two whole pizzas and one third of another pizza. The pizzas are cut into thirds."
> *Draws 2 full circles and ⅓ of another circle.*
> "What's the **mixed number**? **2 and ⅓**. We write **2 ⅓**."
> "Now, let's count the slices! One whole pizza is **3 thirds**. So two whole pizzas are **6 thirds**. Plus the other **1 third**. That's **7 thirds**! So the **improper fraction** is **7/3**."
> "**2 ⅓** and **7/3** are the same amount!"

---

#### **3. Addressing Common Misconceptions**

*   **Misconception:** "A bigger bottom number (denominator) means a bigger fraction."  
    **Clarification:** "Remember, the bottom number *only* tells us how many pieces *one whole* is cut into. When we have improper fractions, the top number is so big because we are counting *many pieces* from *many wholes*. **5/2** (five pieces) is more than **3/2** (three pieces), even though they are both halves."
*   **Misconception:** Thinking ⅗ is bigger than ⅗ because 5>3.  
    **Clarification:** Use the pizza analogy immediately. "Which is more pizza: 3 slices from a pizza cut into 5 pieces, or 5 slices from a pizza cut into 5 pieces? Right! 5 slices is a whole pizza! So ⅗ is actually larger."

---

#### **4. Interactive Activity: Pizza Box Collage (15 mins)**

**Instructions:**
1.  "At your tables, you are going to create your own pizza party order!"
2.  Give each student a piece of construction paper ("the pizza box"), several pre-cut pizza circles (halves, thirds, fourths), a glue stick, and scissors.
3.  "Your job is to glue down enough pizza slices to show one of these orders:"
    *   **Order A:** 5/4 (five fourths)
    *   **Order B:** 1 ¾ (one and three-fourths)
    *   **Order C:** 5/2 (five halves)
    *   **Order D:** 2 ⅓ (two and one-third)
4.  "Once you've glued down your slices neatly in your 'box,' on the lid, write the order in *both* forms—the mixed number AND the improper fraction."
5.  Students work in pairs or small groups to discuss and build their pizzas. The teacher circulates to ask guiding questions: "How many wholes can you make from those slices? How many are left over?"
