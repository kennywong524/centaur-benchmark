**Grade 3 Lesson Segment: Improper Fractions and Mixed Numbers**

---

### **Learning Objective**

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:
- Tell what an improper fraction is.
- Change an improper fraction to a mixed number, and a mixed number to an improper fraction.

---

### **Simple Explanation**

- **Fraction:** A way to show parts of a whole.  
  Example: If you cut a pizza into 4 equal slices and eat 1, you ate **1/4** of the pizza.

- **Proper Fraction:** The top number (numerator) is smaller than the bottom number (denominator).  
  Example: **2/5** (2 is less than 5)

- **Improper Fraction:** The top number is **equal to or bigger** than the bottom number.  
  Example: **5/4** (5 is more than 4)

- **Mixed Number:** A number that has a **whole number and a fraction** together.  
  Example: **1 1/4** (reads “one and one-fourth”)

---

### **Analogy**

**Think of a box of crayons!**

- Each box holds **4 crayons**.
- If you have **5 crayons**, you can fill one whole box (4 crayons), and you have 1 crayon left over.

So, **5 crayons** is **1 full box and 1 extra crayon**.

---

### **Worked Examples**

#### **Example 1: Improper Fraction to Mixed Number**

**5/4** (5 crayons, 4 fit in a box)

- How many full boxes? **5 ÷ 4 = 1** full box.
- How many left over? **1** crayon left.
- Write as a mixed number: **1 1/4** (1 whole box and 1 out of 4 in the next box).

#### **Example 2: Mixed Number to Improper Fraction**

**2 3/5** (2 whole boxes, each holds 5 crayons, plus 3 more crayons)

- 2 whole boxes: **2 × 5 = 10** crayons
- Add the 3 extra crayons: **10 + 3 = 13**
- So, **2 3/5** is the same as **13/5**.

---

### **Common Misconceptions**

- **Mistake:** Thinking 5/4 is less than 1 because the bottom number is bigger.
  - **Truth:** If the top number is bigger, the fraction is **more than 1 whole!**
- **Mistake:** Forgetting to add the extra part when changing a mixed number to an improper fraction.

---

### **Interactive Activity**

**Crayon Box Game (with paper visuals):**

1. Give each student a set of “crayon” cutouts and “boxes” (each box holds 4 crayons).
2. Say a number, like **9/4**.
3. Students fill boxes with 4 crayons each, then see how many full boxes and how many extra crayons.
4. Write the mixed number (**2 1/4**) and the improper fraction (**9/4**) on the board.

---

### **Checks for Understanding**

Ask students:

- **What is 7/3 as a mixed number?**  
  (Answer: 2 1/3)

- **How would you write 1 2/5 as an improper fraction?**  
  (Answer: 7/5)

- **If you have 11/4 crayons, how many full boxes and how many left over?**  
  (Answer: 2 full boxes, 3 left over, so 2 3/4)

---

### **Wrap-Up**

Let’s remember:

- An **improper fraction** has a top number as big or bigger than the bottom.
- A **mixed number** has a whole number and a fraction.
- We can switch between them by seeing how many “whole boxes” we can fill and how many are left over.

**Next time you see a fraction like 9/4, ask yourself: How many full boxes and how many extras?**
