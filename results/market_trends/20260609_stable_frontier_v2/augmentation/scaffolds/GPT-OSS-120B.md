**U.S. Natural‑Gas Market – 2026 Outlook (Briefing)**  

---

### Bullish (price‑supporting) Trends  

| # | Trend | Why it pushes prices higher (2026) |
|---|-------|-----------------------------------|
| 1 | **Tight winter‑season supply** | A colder‑than‑average December‑February in the Midwest and Northeast drives higher heating demand while new production growth remains modest. Inventories are projected to finish the season ~10 % below the five‑year average, tightening the market. |
| 2 | **Accelerated LNG export growth** | Two new U.S. export terminals (e.g., Baytown‑2 and Corpus Christi‑3) are expected to be in service by mid‑2026, raising annual LNG capacity by ~7 bcf. Strong overseas demand (Europe’s energy‑security contracts and Asian summer‑peak loads) creates a premium for U.S. gas on the spot market, pulling domestic prices up. |
| 3 | **Policy‑driven demand from clean‑energy transition** | The Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) and state clean‑energy standards continue to incentivize natural‑gas‑fired combined‑cycle plants as “bridge” resources for variable renewables. New gas‑back‑up capacity and the expansion of hydrogen‑blending projects add roughly 2 bcf/d of incremental demand by year‑end. |

---

### Bearish (price‑depressing) Trends  

| # | Trend | Why it pushes prices lower (2026) |
|---|-------|-----------------------------------|
| 1 | **Record‑high domestic production** | The Permian Basin and Haynesville continue to deliver record output; 2026 dry‑gas production is forecast at ~103 bcf/d, the highest in a decade. This surplus feeds the spot market and keeps forward curves in contango. |
| 2 | **Mild summer weather and high storage levels** | The June‑August period is projected to be 5‑10 % cooler than the 30‑year norm, reducing power‑generation demand. Combined with the already‑full 2025‑2026 storage cycle (≈1.3 Tcf in underground caverns), the market faces abundant supply. |
| 3 | **Regulatory headwinds on new pipelines** | Ongoing litigation and the Biden administration’s “climate‑risk” review process delay key mid‑continent pipeline projects (e.g., the Gulf‑Coast Expansion). Reduced mid‑continent transport capacity limits the ability to move gas to higher‑priced Gulf and export markets, dampening price appreciation. |

---

### How the Factors Interact  

- **Supply vs. Demand:** Record production (bearish) is offset by rising export‑driven demand and policy‑backed power‑plant builds (bullish). The net effect hinges on whether new LNG capacity can absorb the surplus.  
- **Weather & Storage:** A cold winter depletes inventories, reinforcing bullish pressure, while a mild summer refills them, creating a seasonal swing that can be exploited by traders.  
- **Infrastructure & Policy:** Pipeline bottlenecks (bearish) limit the flow of gas to export terminals, but the commissioning of new LNG facilities (bullish) provides an alternative outlet, partially mitigating the bottleneck. Clean‑energy incentives (bullish) generate incremental demand that can absorb excess production, but only if the regulatory environment permits timely construction of related infrastructure.  

---

### Bottom‑Line Outlook (Investors/Companies)

Overall, 2026 is likely to be a **volatile year for U.S. natural gas**, with price support from a cold winter, expanding LNG export capacity, and clean‑energy‑driven demand, but offset by record production, mild summer weather, and pipeline‑approval delays. Investors should position for **seasonal price spikes** while maintaining exposure to **mid‑term fundamentals** that favor a modestly bullish price trajectory as export markets mature. Companies that can flexibly shift gas between domestic power markets and LNG export contracts will be best positioned to capture upside while managing downside risk.
