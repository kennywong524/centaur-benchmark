Bullish trends (likely to push U.S. natural gas prices up in 2026)
1. Rising LNG export volumes and global demand — new and ramping U.S. liquefaction trains tie domestic gas markets to higher-priced global markets, tightening domestic balances when export flows are strong.  
2. Slower U.S. upstream growth / capital discipline — continued focus on returns, higher breakevens in some basins, and well decline dynamics can limit incremental supply growth and elevate baseline tightness.  
3. Weather-driven demand spikes and lower-than-expected storage withdrawals — episodic cold snaps (or hotter-than-normal summers raising power demand for cooling) combined with tighter storage replenishment can produce short-term price rallies.

Bearish trends (likely to push U.S. natural gas prices down in 2026)
1. Continued production resilience and associated gas — oil-directed drilling and efficiency gains can keep associated and shale gas volumes strong, flattening upward price pressure.  
2. Tepid industrial demand / milder seasonal weather — weaker macro activity or a milder winter/shoulder seasons reduce consumption, leaving larger storage buffers and downward price pressure.  
3. Infrastructure and policy easing long-term demand or bottlenecks — pipeline expansions, faster permitting of takeaway capacity, more aggressive electrification/renewables and efficiency policies can relieve regional tightness and reduce future gas demand growth.

How these factors interact (brief)
- Supply and infrastructure interact directly: takeaway limits or new pipeline/LNG capacity determine whether incremental U.S. production reaches markets or is locally discounted.  
- LNG exports connect domestic storage and spot balances to global demand; higher export flows accelerate winter/shoulder drawdowns and amplify weather sensitivity.  
- Weather is the immediate swing factor for demand and storage levels; storage inventories set the starting point going into high-demand periods and modulate price volatility.  
- Policy (permitting, methane rules, clean-energy incentives) shapes investment incentives in both upstream supply and gas-fired demand (power-sector retirements or gas-to-clean switching), influencing medium-term fundamentals.

Overall outlook (2–3 sentences)
Natural gas markets in 2026 are likely to remain volatile, with upward price risk from LNG growth, production discipline, and weather shocks balanced by supply resilience, milder demand scenarios, and structural shifts from infrastructure and policy. Investors and companies should prioritize flexible exposure, active hedging, and attention to regional infrastructure constraints and policy developments to manage both upside opportunities and downside risks.
