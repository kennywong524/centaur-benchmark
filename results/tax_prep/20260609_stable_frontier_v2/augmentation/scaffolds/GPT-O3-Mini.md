1. FREELANCE INCOME, SCHEDULE C, AND SELF‑EMPLOYMENT TAX

A. Discrepancy in Reported Income  
• The client’s 1099‑NEC shows freelance income of $4,200 but the Form 1040 only shows $2,400.  
• Correction: The full $4,200 must be reported on a Schedule C (Profit or Loss from Business).  
  – In the absence of business expense data, you would assume zero expenses (or ask the client exactly what the expenses are).  
  – The net profit from Schedule C becomes $4,200 (or less if expenses are provided).

B. Self‑Employment Tax (Schedule SE)  
• The current return omits Schedule SE and no self‑employment tax is being computed.  
• Rule: The net earnings from self‑employment are subject to SE tax. (Recall that, typically, you multiply the net profit by 92.35% and then apply a 15.3% tax rate.)  
  For example, if no expenses are allowed, Net Earnings = 0.9235 × $4,200 ≈ $3,880  
  SE Tax ≈ 15.3% × $3,880 ≈ $594  
• Also, half the SE tax (≈$297) is normally deductible as an adjustment to income on Form 1040.

C. Required Forms/Schedules  
• Prepare a Schedule C to report the full freelance/contract income (and then subtract allowed business expenses once provided).  
• Prepare a Schedule SE to compute self‑employment tax.  
• On Form 1040, include the deduction for ½ SE tax.

3. DEPENDENT STATUS, CREDITS, AND FILING STATUS

A. Dependent’s Classification for Credits  
• The taxpayer is claiming a 22‑year‑old full‑time college student as a “child” for the Child Tax Credit.  
• Federal rules require that the qualifying child be under age 17 at the end of the tax year for the Child Tax Credit.  
  – A 22‑year‑old does not qualify for the Child Tax Credit but might be claimed as a “qualifying relative” who is eligible for the Credit for Other Dependents (worth up to $500, subject to current law).  
• Correction: The return should be changed to claim the Credit for Other Dependents on Form 1040 rather than the Child Tax Credit for this dependent.

B. Filing Status – Single vs. Head of Household  
• The client selected “Single.” However, if the taxpayer is unmarried and “paid more than half the cost of maintaining a home” in which a qualifying dependent lived for more than half the year, the taxpayer may file as Head of Household.  
• Under the rules for Head of Household, a dependent who is a full‑time college student under age 24 (in this case, age 22) qualifies as a “qualifying person.”  
• In this situation, if the taxpayer indeed paid more than half the household costs and the student lived with the taxpayer for at least 6 months (9 months in this case), then the taxpayer should be filing as Head of Household—not Single.
  
C. Required Correction  
• On Form 1040, change the filing status from Single to Head of Household.  
• Adjust any credits (and possibly tax rates) that change because of the proper filing status.  
• Review the dependent section to claim the Credit for Other Dependents rather than a Child Tax Credit.
